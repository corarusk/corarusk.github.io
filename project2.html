Problem

cost & clutter

What prevents people from decorating their homes for seasons and holidays?

The amount of storage space people have in their homes is limited. Not only is their lack of space to store decor items in the off-season an issue, decorating is expensive! People are in a lose-win-lose cycle of irony: Spend money to make your home feel more “homey” and now your closets are overflowing with STUFF causing so much clutter it doesn’t feel “homey” after all. Doesn’t make much sense, right?

People are wasting precious space and money purchasing home decor items that they will only use for one month out of the year and are in need of an easy solution.

Solution

season.ly

A mobile IOS application that helps people affordably decorate their home for seasons and holidays without all the clutter.

how it works



Ideation

IDENTIFYING A PROBLEM

With the seasons changing and the holidays approaching, I started to daydream and envision decorating my house for Fall. There's something so soothing about a warm color palette and the smell of pumpkin spice.

So, one night I was window shopping at Target and saw the cutest Fall decor. It took every bone in my body not to put the whole end-cap in my cart. At that moment, I found myself wishing I lived in a bigger house. I knew if I brought two armfulls of shopping bags home my husband would say, "Where the heck are we going to put all that stuff? Truth is, he would be right and I would have no argument.

Then, the question hit me... if I'm having this problem, then are others too? Good thing asking questions - and finding answers - is my game.

questions that need answers

  • Which seasons and holidays do users enjoy decorating their home for the most?

  • What barriers prevent users from decorating their home?

HIGHLIGHTS

of participants spend more than $40 per year on home decor

of participants store decor items in their closets when not in season


validated assumptions

  • Amount of storage space is an issue

  • Seasonal and holiday decor is popular and common

  • Users want to decorate but think it’s too expensive

  • Users want to customize their items in a kit

  • Users don’t find subscription services to be convenient or “hassle-free”

  • Users dislike clutter

  • Users think returns are complicated when online shopping

  • Free shipping label and container are a must for returns


new findings

  • Fall and Winter are the most popular seasons for home decor

  • Halloween, Thanksgiving, and Christmas are most popular holidays for home decor

  • Living room, front porch, kitchen, entryway (in that order) are user’s favorite rooms to decorate

  • Users think shopping is too much work; they don’t feel like going out or looking for items to buy

  • Store locator is not a priority for returns

User Personas

The creation of accurate user personas is essential to designing a product that solves a real problem. Using research and survey data, I created two main Personas to represent the users of this product.

Sarah "the Independent Renter"

Age 26 / Marketing Assistant

Goals

  • Decorate her home for seasons and additional holidays to make her rental feel more like a home

  • Limit the amount of items she owns in order to gain control back of her closet space

  • Increase organization and reduce clutter to make packing and unpacking easy for her next move

Frustrations

  • Feels cluttered and doesn’t have any room to store decor when it’s not in use for the season

  • Home decor is so expensive and feels like she is wasting money if she doesn’t use it year-round

  • Doesn’t like some of the items she receives in her Fab Fit Fun subscription and wishes there were more customization options online

cindy "the social homeowner"

Age 39 / Pediatrician

Goals

  • Change out the same, old home decorations she has been resusing for years

  • Make her home feel festive for holiday parties and family gatherings

  • Reduce clutter in the garage so her and her husband can both park their cars inside this winter

Frustrations

  • Using too much space in the garage for storage of items when not in use

  • Finding decor that matches online is too time consuming and doesn’t know if it will look good in her house when it's all put together

  • Doesn’t have enough time to go shopping in store and hates fighting crowds

User Stories

DEFINING THE SCOPE

Research detailed thus far has helped me better understand the user’s needs and create a Minimum Viable Product (MVP). User stories were created to prioritize tasks in regards to the needs of the users and create a reference throughout the project to ensure a consistent scope.


MVP

Users want an affordable and hassle-free way to decorate their home for seasons and holidays. Users want to rent a preassembled kit for quick shopping and they also want the option to customize their kit for their style, home, and budget needs.


high priority tasks

  • Sign up for an account

  • Sign in to my account

  • Browse decor kits by season or holiday

  • Customize an assembled kit

  • View a list of items included in the kit

  • Rent a kit that is pre-assembled

  • Understand the delivery and return shipping process

  • Receive a confirmation

  • Choose from different sizes

Wireframes & Usability Testing

LOW FIDELITY

First, I roughly sketched out the layouts of all of the pages in a notebook. To decide on layouts, I used my personal version of Crazy 8’s core design sprint method to push myself beyond my first idea and determine the best layout quickly.




Next, I hopped right in to Balsamiq to create wireframes of the onboarding process quickly, because I knew that was the flow I wanted to test first.

Usability Testing Round 1

The main area I was looking for feedback on was the “Sign in or Create Account” button. I combined these steps by having the user type in their email regardless if they have an account or not (because either way they enter their email address, so why add an extra step?) I printed the PDFs exported from Balsamiq and facilitated a usability test with the paper prototype.





Task

  • Create an account

test findings

  • The button is suggested to be intuitive, but a different pain point is revealed. The “create password” form at the top of screen surprised the user and caused them to go from the bottom (email) to the top (password).

    • Result → Move the enter password field down to resemble the same layout as when they entered their email. This allows for a more seamless transition between screens. Onward!!



medium FIDELITY

My main goal is identifying any complications in the UX as quickly as possible, so I needed to get the digital wireframes done fast. Making use of components, I was easily able to create medium-fidelity wireframes for the whole flow in just a couple of hours.

Usability Testing Rounds 2-3

More testing! I created an InVision Prototype and facilitated one in-person and one live remote test. Between each test, a series of iterations were completed. I also deployed a usability test on Maze that gathered data from 13 users.





Tasks

  • Create an account

  • Browse and select a kit

  • Customize a medium-sixed kit

  • Checkout and place your order

test findings

  • Users had a hard time understanding that they were able to customize a kit after choosing a size.

    • Result → Add a fixed footer to prevent the user needing to scroll down.



  • After customizing a kit, the user wanted to keep shopping, but was only given the option to replace another item or continue to checkout. With this iteration, there was no choice to finish customizing the kit and shop for another kit.

    • Result → Change "continue to checkout" to "done", which takes the user back to view their customized kit.
    Result → Once the kit is added to cart, a confirmation appears and gives users a choice to continue shopping or checkout.

Key Takeaways

  • Get the first iterations of wireframes done quickly to deploy testing in early stages of the design process.
  • LISTEN TO USERS! → Test. Repeat. Test. Repeat.

Concept & Identity

COLOR PALETTE

Season.ly is trendy, festive, reliable, relaxing, and most importantly … feels like home. Carefully chosen to create the same environment as the brand characteristics, shades of seam foam blue are used to represent trustworthiness and the dark maroon is warm, inviting, and energetic. The colors contrast against each other to represent the contrast of seasons in the year.




logo

I started brainstorming with a good old-fashioned pencil and paper. The period in the middle of the word plays on the door knob on the front door of the home in the logo. The script 'S' is trendy and creative, like the brand.




typography

The brand makes use of two typefaces - Playfair Display for the brand name and main headers, paired with a combination of uppercase and sentence case Work Sans for labels and paragraph text. Like the colors, these typefaces are chosen because they are very different from one another (again representing contrast of seasons). Playfair Display is also very elegant and trendy, just like Season.ly.

HIGH FIDELITY MOCKUPS

Using Figma, I turned the wireframes into high fidelity mockups. Consistency is key for great usability, so I implemented the design system I created for my style guide to reuse components across pages and adhere to spacing guidelines. A design system ensures consistency in the interface across every page.







An interesting challenge

One of the most challenging designs, aside from the customization flow, was the cart details (checkout) page. There is a lot of information, so I needed to take a step back, view this from a user’s perspective, and determine which information and actions were most important.

For example, the list of items included in the kit is hidden on the checkout page. This decision was made so the information does not distract users. If a user would like to be reminded of the items in the kit, they can toggle the “view details” dropdown list to show and hide the information, as needed.

Prototype & User Testing

Analyzing user behavior

I created a prototype in Invision to test the usability of the application in high fidelity. Two in-person usability tests were conducted so that I could witness the user’s behavior and mannerisms while navigating through the tasks.

Because data is so important, I also created an online version of the usability test on Maze that collected results from 7 other users. Maze creates heat maps for each screen to show where users clicked and the path users took to complete the tasks, which is extremely helpful in understanding user behavior.


Tasks

  • Create an account

  • Browse and select a kit

  • Customize a medium-sixed kit

  • Checkout and place your order

Notice the single click that suggests that the user did not have any confusion or difficulties creating an account.



On the contrary, choosing a medium-sized kit was rather confusing to users. The heatmap shows that users were clicking on the checkmarks in the image (which are not yet active). This data suggests that the checkmarks on the image are distracting the user from the actual task at hand - choose a kit size.

what works

  • Onboarding - Creating an account

  • Home - Browsing by category

  • Checkout - Placing an order






what needs work

  • Viewing product details and customization (choosing a kit size and replacing an item) is difficult. Users are confused about the active state of the item on the kit size page, meaning when the item can be clicked on to be replaced and when it can’t.

Iterations

How can I make it clear to users that they must press the “customize kit” button before the items are able to be clicked on to be replaced?

In order to make iterations to the customization feature of the app, I conducted more research of online shopping products.

What are other products doing and does it work?

VIEW PRODUCT DETAILS FEATURE

Instagram has a very effective way of viewing items within an image. After gathering opinions from others that have used the shopping feature on Instagram, I decided to use their flow as inspiration.




Subtle white highlights cue, "Hey, you, click on me!" because I don't EVER want to leave users guessing during their experience. A little animation goes a long way.





CUSTOMIZATION FEATURE

Nike allows users to customize a pair of shoes and has a really clear way of showing that the item has been customized. I used the similar “by you” phrase.

Refinements

Another round of research

We never run out of things to research, we just run out of time. Now that the project is completed, I was able to dig a little deeper into research topics that I didn't have enough time to explore in the discovery phase.

questions

What other products offer a rental service?
How do they protect themselves against damages?
How do they convey their promise?

Discovery #1

Rent the Runway, a clothing rental service, provides the user with a list of details and information about the product before they decide to add it to their cart. Great inspiration for how to convey the brand’s trustworthiness.

In order to help my users feel confident and secure about renting a Season.ly kit, I’ve added two dropdown sections: “Our Promise” and “Rental Policy”.





Discovery #2

Rental services usually offer rental insurance. Frillest, for example, is optional. Rent the Runway is required.

I’ve added a $5 optional insurance fee users can purchase to cover any possible damage to the items and general wear-and-tear. This is a smart business strategy to cover any legal issues that may arise with a rental service.





Design Critique

The concept of a fixed footer initially came to light in the wireframing stage, but in the visual design stage, the cta buttons seemed to be more visually appealing larger. While there were not any issues that arose during the final round of user testing, I received feedback from a Senior Designer that a fixed footer would improve the usability by decreasing the user’s cognitive load. For example, with a fixed footer, user’s do not need to think about where the buttons are, they are visible at all times. Open and accepting to feedback, I made iterations to the final design by adding a fixed footer ("Customize" and "Add to Cart")

Stay driven and determined

One of the most challenging parts of this project was the customization feature that I was determined to add. Research proved that users NEED the ability to customize, but figuring out how to make it happen was the tricky part. Ultimately, the answers were found by brainstorming with other designers, continuous research, and multiple rounds of user testing.

learning never stops

Design is interesting. Just when you think you have nailed down a solution, it seems there are more challenges that get thrown in our way. It could be easy to get discouraged when you think you’ve found it, then after testing find out it doesn’t work. But design isn’t easy and nobody is perfect. We try and if we fall down, we get up and try again. And boy, does it heighten that feeling you get when you FINALLY solve it!

Key Takeaways

  • Be encouraged to bounce ideas off of colleagues. The best ideas come to light when you put more than one head together.
  • Research is ongoing. Be sure to explore every possibility and don’t stop trying until you get there.
